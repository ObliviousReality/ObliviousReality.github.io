Brief Introduction:

This is a collection of stories and snippets of some of the work I have produced whilst at the University of Lancaster. The University's rules mean that I am not allowed to share code with others, as it is a breach of copyright, but I can still discuss the projects I have completed, and I will discuss the various tools I have used and what I have produced.

Dissertation:

My Dissertation, completed during my Third Year of University covered the use of Machine Learning Classification to predict whether a program fault (i.e. bug) could be fixed automatically using an Automated Repair Program. This was not the dissertation that I wanted to do, I originally requested projects around Game Development or Software Development, but in the end I feel I produced a strong and potentially useful finding for the field of Automated Program Repair (APR).
APR is a fairly new concept of using Genetic Programming to automatically fix faults in software, but has only had limited sucess over the last 10 years of its existance. My dissertation aimed to automatically classify these bugs into either Fixable or Not Fixable to both speed up the APR process and also to produce a dataset that can be analysed to see if patterns or trends are visible to explain why some bugs cannot be fixed and some can.
My project had mixed success in these regards, while I managed to produce a classification program that was capable of pretty accurately classifying the bugs into the categories, the data produced from this did not show any useful patterns or trends.
I got to use a swathe of new software during this project, with my first exposure to tools like Docker, a useful alternative to a VM, and Weka, a useful machine learning platform with a good user interface as well as programmatable options. I plan to use both of these in future projects. I was given freedom to use whatever language I liked, and I ended up using Python for the large majority of this project as I had to work with files a lot, and Python is just far superior than any other langauges when working with files. I also chose to use Microsoft Excel over statistical languages like R for analysis as I am more comfortable with these, and I have definitely improved my Excel skills, especially with Pivot Tables and large dataset managment. I even got to use some SQL to combined these datasets together.
As for the field of Automated Program Repair, while I eventually found the way it works interesting and saw its potential, for me this field just isn't there yet for me to get involved with it or want to investigate my findings further. I'm still set on my original interests of Software and Game Development.

Distributed Systems

My favourite module studied during my Third Year was covering Distributed Systems. While the course content itself was not the most interesting or engaging, I was mostly swayed by its coursework. The Module, 311, had one main coursework over 10 weeks, split up into a few marking sessiosn to track progress, and the aim was to produce a networked Client-Server auctioning system. Again, not the most exciting but it was the most 'free' coursework in terms of implementation I had had in the whole degree. As CS coursework is hard to make 'harder', the department scaled difficulty by removing the amount of support and hand-holding they offered, so in first year a coursework would just be a slight spin on something we had worked through in a lab, whereas in this coursework, we were gived a bried, told which libraries could help, and told to meet a set of guidelines by the given due date.
